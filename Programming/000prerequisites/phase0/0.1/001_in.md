# Phase 0.1A — Matter, Atoms, Electricity, Electronics, and Information

## Introduction

Before we can understand bits, binary numbers, memory, CPUs, or software, we must understand what physical reality a computer is built from.

A computer is not magic.

A computer is made from matter.

Matter is made from atoms.

Atoms contain electrons.

Electrons create electricity.

Electricity enables electronics.

Electronics enable bits.

Bits enable computation.

Everything in computer science ultimately traces back to physics.

---

Examples:

- Your keyboard
- Your laptop
- Your phone

- Your body
- The air around you

All are made of atoms.

Atoms are the basic building blocks of matter.

Think of atoms as tiny LEGO blocks from which the universe is constructed.

---

# Structure Of An Atom

An atom contains three primary particles.

| Particle | Charge       |
| -------- | ------------ |
| Proton   | Positive (+) |
| Neutron  | Neutral (0)  |
| Electron | Negative (-) |

A simplified picture looks like:

```text
        Electron (-)

             ○

             |
             |

     [ Proton + Neutron ]
          Nucleus

             |
             |

             ○

        Electron (-)
```

The center is called the nucleus.

The nucleus contains:

- Protons
- Neutrons

Electrons exist around the nucleus.

---

# What Creates Electricity?

Of the three particles:

- Protons are mostly locked inside the nucleus.
- Neutrons remain inside the nucleus.
- Electrons can move.

Electricity is fundamentally the movement of electrons.

That's it.

Everything electrical eventually reduces to:

```text
Electrons moving
```

No movement:

```text
No electrical current
```

Movement:

```text
Electrical current
```

---

# What Is Electrical Current?

Current measures how many electrons flow through a conductor.

Think about water flowing through a pipe.

Water analogy:

```text
Water flow rate
```

Electricity analogy:

```text
Electron flow rate
```

The more electrons moving per second:

```text
Higher current
```

The fewer electrons moving per second:

```text
Lower current
```

Current is measured in:

```text
Amperes (Amps)
```

Usually written as:

```text
A
```

---

# What Is One Amp?

One Amp means:

```text
Approximately

6,242,000,000,000,000,000 electrons

pass a point every second.
```

Or:

```text
6.242 × 10^18 electrons per second
```

This number is unimaginably large.

Even a tiny electrical current involves enormous numbers of electrons.

---

# What Is Voltage?

Current tells us:

```text
How many electrons move
```

Voltage tells us:

```text
How strongly they are pushed
```

Voltage is electrical pressure.

Think about water pipes.

Imagine two pipes:

Pipe A:

```text
Low pressure
```

Pipe B:

```text
High pressure
```

Water in Pipe B pushes much harder.

Voltage works similarly.

Higher voltage means:

```text
Stronger push on electrons
```

Lower voltage means:

```text
Weaker push on electrons
```

Voltage is measured in:

```text
Volts (V)
```

---

# Voltage vs Current

Many beginners confuse these.

Think:

| Water System | Electrical System |
| ------------ | ----------------- |
| Pressure     | Voltage           |
| Water Flow   | Current           |

Voltage:

```text
How hard the push is
```

Current:

```text
How much stuff is moving
```

These are different concepts.

You can have:

```text
High voltage
Low current
```

or

```text
Low voltage
High current
```

---

# Does More Voltage Mean More Electrons?

Not necessarily.

Voltage does not directly tell us how many electrons move.

Voltage tells us how strongly they want to move.

The actual number of electrons moving depends on:

- Voltage
- Material
- Resistance
- Circuit design

Think:

```text
More voltage
↓
Stronger push
↓
Potentially more current
↓
More electrons moving

-----

Think of water pipes:

Voltage = water pressure
Current = liters per second flowing
Resistance = pipe width

You can have:

Massive pressure
Tiny pipe

Result:

High pressure
Small flow

Exactly like:

High voltage
Low current
```

But voltage alone does not determine current.

---

# What Is Resistance?

Not all materials allow electrons to move easily.

Examples:

Good conductors:

- Copper
- Silver
- Gold

Poor conductors:

- Rubber
- Plastic
- Wood

Resistance measures how difficult it is for electrons to move.

High resistance:

```text
Harder for electrons to flow
```

Low resistance:

```text
Easier for electrons to flow
```

# The Three Fundamental Electrical Quantities

Every electrical system can be understood using:

| Quantity   | Meaning    |
| ---------- | ---------- |
| Voltage    | Push       |
| Current    | Flow       |
| Resistance | Difficulty |

These three concepts explain most basic electronics.

# Electrical vs Electronic

These words sound similar but mean different things.

## Electrical Systems

Electrical systems focus on:

```text
Moving power
```

Examples:

- Power lines
- Electric motors
- Generators
- Home wiring

Goal:

```text
Deliver energy
```

---

## Electronic Systems

Electronic systems focus on:

```text
Controlling information
```

Examples:

- CPUs
- RAM
- GPUs
- Smartphones
- Computers

Goal:

```text
Process information
```

A power station is electrical.

A CPU is electronic.

# Why Electronics Changed The World

Humans discovered something remarkable.

If we carefully control the movement of electrons, we can represent information.

For example:

```text
No voltage
↓
0

Voltage present
↓
1
```

Or:

```text
Low voltage
↓
0

High voltage
↓
1
```

Now physical reality can represent information.

This is the foundation of computing.
